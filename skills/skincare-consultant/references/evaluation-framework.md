# Product evaluation framework

## Verify the candidate

Capture:

- Exact product and brand
- Product type and intended area
- Market/region and date checked
- Full ingredient source
- Packaging or formulation version when available
- Confidence: high, medium, or low

Source priority:

1. Current packaging supplied by the user for the exact item
2. Official regional brand product page
3. Official brand support or published formula documentation
4. Reputable regional retailer
5. Independent ingredient database or review

If sources conflict, show the conflict and lower confidence. Do not combine lists.

## Map function and routine role

Classify the product by both routine role and actual functions. Examples include:

- Cleanser or makeup/sunscreen removal
- Humectant hydration, emollience, occlusion, or barrier support
- Sunscreen
- Exfoliation: AHA, BHA, PHA, enzymes, or physical exfoliation
- Retinoid/retinal/retinol treatment
- Acne support
- Pigment or tone support
- Antioxidant support
- Soothing or redness-support claims
- Cosmetic finish, tone-up, or makeup support

Marketing categories are not evidence of a distinct function.

## Compare against the inventory

Evaluate overlap at three levels:

1. **Active overlap**: the same or closely related meaningful active.
2. **Goal overlap**: different ingredients targeting the same concern.
3. **Role overlap**: multiple products occupying the same routine step without a clear advantage.

For every overlap, name the existing product and classify it as:

- Direct substitute
- Partial substitute
- Complementary
- Cumulative irritation risk
- No meaningful overlap

Do not count common base ingredients such as water, glycerin, or standard emollients as problematic duplication by themselves.

## Assess cumulative risk

Consider concentration when known, vehicle, frequency, treated area, current barrier state, prior tolerance, and the rest of the routine. Distinguish:

- Documented contraindication or clinician restriction
- Likely additive irritation or dryness
- Redundant exposure with limited extra benefit
- Theoretical interaction without useful evidence
- Personal sensory or cosmetic incompatibility such as pilling

Avoid rigid internet-style rules that declare all acids, vitamin C products, niacinamide, peptides, or retinoids mutually incompatible.

## Assign a verdict

- **Good fit**: addresses a priority goal, fits the profile and routine, and adds meaningful value with manageable risk.
- **Optional**: reasonable but nonessential, preference-driven, or only a small improvement over existing options.
- **Redundant**: duplicates an owned product or routine role without sufficient added value.
- **Use cautiously**: may help, but tolerance, active load, health context, or formula uncertainty requires conservative use or professional input.
- **Avoid**: known allergy/reaction, clear clinician restriction, inappropriate use area, unacceptable risk, or a red-flag situation. Explain the exact reason.

## Standard output

```markdown
## Verdict: [Good fit | Optional | Redundant | Use cautiously | Avoid]

[One- or two-sentence decision, including the most important safety point.]

### Fit with the profile
- ...

### Key ingredients and functions
- ...

### Inventory overlap
- Existing product — [direct substitute / partial substitute / complementary / cumulative risk]

### Safety and routine placement
- Suggested step and conservative starting frequency, if appropriate
- Monitoring and stop criteria

### Purchase recommendation
[Buy / sample / finish an existing product first / skip]

### Confidence
[High / Medium / Low], because ...
Missing: ...
```

Do not invent a routine placement or frequency when product identity, formula, or safety context is insufficient.
